Broc vs screen-time limits: why timers are easy to ignore

You set a 30-minute limit on Instagram. Three days later you're tapping "Ignore limit for today" without reading it. This page explains why that keeps happening and what a blocker has to do differently to survive contact with 11pm you.

The problem: you hold the snooze button

Every built-in screen-time tool works the same way. It counts your minutes, and when they run out, it asks you to stop. Asking is the flaw. The same person who set the limit gets to waive it, and the waive button is one tap while the discipline costs actual effort. When willpower is low, that trade loses every time.

It's not a fair fight either. The apps you're limiting are built by teams whose whole job is keeping you in them. A polite dialog box was never going to win. Your screen-time report is a scoreboard for a game you're losing.

What actually changes behavior: a price, not a plea

A limit that can be dismissed for free is a suggestion. Behavior changes when the easy path has a real cost and the good path has a real payoff. That means the block can't end with a tap. It has to end with something you'd have to physically do.

Common questions

Can't you just uninstall it?

Yes. Broc doesn't pretend to be a prison. The point is asymmetry: tapping "5 more minutes" costs nothing, uninstalling an app you deliberately set up is a decision, and your streak makes that decision visible. A pause is easy to outlast. Thirty pushups are not.

How is this different from one-sec-style pause apps?

Pause apps make you wait or breathe before opening the app. The exit still costs nothing but time. Broc's exit is doing the habit you picked.

Is it free?

Broc is a subscription with a 7-day free trial. One plan, everything in: unlimited habits, custom unlock durations, AI photo verification and full history. No ads.

What about privacy?

Broc uses Android's Usage Access permission only to check which app is open right now, on your device. No usage history is stored or sent. Details in the privacy policy.
