What is a habit app blocker?

A blocker that doesn't count minutes. It locks your apps until your real habits are done, and hands the time back as a reward.

The one-line definition

A habit app blocker locks your distracting apps until you complete real-world habits. No countdown, no "take a breath" prompt, no ignore button. The feed opens when the reading, the workout, or the studying is done, because that's the deal you set.

Broc is a habit app blocker for Android. You pick the apps that pull you in (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, games), set your daily habits, and every finished habit earns minutes of screen time. Open a blocked app early and you see your habit list instead of the feed.

Why timers keep failing you

Every screen-time tool you've already tried runs on the same engine: measure the minutes, then ask you to stop. Android's Digital Wellbeing, app timers, focus modes. They all share one hole: the person who set the rule can waive the rule, for free, in one tap. When the dialog says "ignore limit for today," the tired version of you at 11pm always wins the argument.

A timer knows how long you scrolled. It has no idea whether you did anything with your day. That's the gap a habit gate closes.

A habit app blocker changes what the exit costs. There is no snooze to hold down. The only way through the wall is doing the thing you already wanted to do. Willpower stops being the mechanism and becomes the byproduct.

How Broc's habit gate works

  • Block: choose any apps on your phone. Opening one shows the wall: your habits and what each one earns.
  • Do the habit: read ten pages, train, meditate, study, walk the dog. Anything you set.
  • Prove it: AI photo verification checks a photo of the finished habit (the open book, the gym). Timer verification counts minutes only while you stay in the app. Your call, per habit. More in the verification guide.
  • Earn it back: per-habit mode gives each habit its own unlock minutes. All-habits-first mode keeps everything locked until the whole list is cleared.
  • Keep score: streaks and a four-week grid per habit turn follow-through into something you can see, and won't want to break.
