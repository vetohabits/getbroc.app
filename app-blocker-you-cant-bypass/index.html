Why you bypass every app blocker

You're not weak. The blockers are. Every one of them ships with an exit, and you've found them all.

By Shay Gavan · Published 12 July 2026 · Reviewed 14 July 2026

The bypass taxonomy

If you've installed and defeated more than one blocker, you'll recognise these. Every mainstream tool falls to at least one:

  • The editable timer. Android's Digital Wellbeing app timers can be removed in Settings before midnight, while Focus mode lets you take a temporary break. The person who set the rule can also waive it.
  • The outlasted delay. Pause apps make you breathe or wait before opening. Day one, profound. Day nine, you tap through the pause on muscle memory while the breathing text is still fading in.
  • The settings walk. Timers and schedules you set can be unset. Thirty seconds in settings and tonight's block never happened.
  • The imaginary stake. Gamified blockers kill a virtual tree when you cave. You mourn the first tree. You do not mourn the fortieth.
  • The quiet uninstall. The exit of last resort, always available, no witnesses.
The common thread: in every case, the lock and the key live in the same pocket. Yours. A block you can waive for free is a suggestion with extra steps.

The uncomfortable truth about "unbypassable"

Broc does not prevent uninstall. Android can technically restrict app removal in managed-device or profile-owner setups, and some strict blockers add settings or uninstall protection. Those controls and their limitations vary by app and device, so an ordinary consumer blocker should not be called impossible to remove.

So the honest question isn't "which blocker has no exit?" None of them. It's "which blocker makes the exit cost more than the habit?" That reframe changes which app wins.

Making the bypass expensive

Three things move the cost of caving above the cost of just doing the thing:

1. Remove the free exits

Broc's wall has no snooze, no "5 more minutes," no ignore button. When you open a blocked app you see your habit list and what each habit earns. The in-app bypass simply doesn't exist, which kills the reflex taps that defeat timers and pauses.

2. Make the unlock require proof

The remaining exit inside the rules is lying: "yep, definitely meditated." Broc closes it with verification. The AI photo check wants to see the open book or the gym before the habit counts; timer verification counts minutes only while you stay in the app. Now the honest path is genuinely the cheapest one available.

3. Give the uninstall a price

Broc leaves uninstall available, so that exit has to feel expensive. It does it with your streak: every day the habits get done, the number grows, and uninstalling means watching day 23 go to zero over one scroll. Editing a timer was a reflex; deleting a three-week streak is a decision. Most nights, the decision loses.

What this looks like in practice

You block Instagram, TikTok and YouTube. Tonight's list: gym, ten pages, tidy the kitchen. At 7pm you reach for Instagram on autopilot and hit the wall: three habits, each with a price next to it. There's no button to argue with, so the argument doesn't happen. You do the gym, snap the photo, and thirty minutes open up. The scroll happens; it just happens after. That ordering, repeated for a month, is the entire product.

Common questions

What about strict modes in other blockers?

Stay Focused and others offer strict modes that lock their own settings, and they do raise the bypass cost. They're still timer logic underneath: when the clock runs out you've earned nothing, learned nothing, and tomorrow starts the same fight. Strictness tied to habits compounds instead; see what a habit app blocker is.

Seriously, what stops me uninstalling Broc?

Nothing mechanical. A visible streak, habits that are finally getting done, and the mild embarrassment of deleting a wall you built yourself. In practice that holds far better than any lock, because it's yours.
